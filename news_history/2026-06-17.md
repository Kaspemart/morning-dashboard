**The Week That Was: A Rollercoaster of Hope, Havoc, and High Tech!**

**🌍 Middle East Meltdown... and Mellowing!**
The week began with intense escalation. On Sunday (June 8), **Iran launched missiles at Israel**, prompting swift Israeli retaliation. Yemen's Iran-backed Houthi rebels joined the fray, launching missile attacks on Israel and declaring a "complete and total ban" on Israeli shipping in the Red Sea. A US military base in Saudi Arabia also came under fire. Tensions spiked further with the downing of a US Apache helicopter near the Strait of Hormuz on Monday, leading to **new US strikes against Iran** on Tuesday (June 10). Iran claimed retaliatory strikes on US bases in Kuwait, Bahrain, and Jordan. The UN Secretary-General warned of a "full war" as Iran's top military command announced the **closure of the Strait of Hormuz** on Wednesday (June 11), even threatening vessels. However, the narrative shifted dramatically when the **US and Iran announced a peace deal** on Monday (June 15), confirmed by Pakistan and the EU, aiming to end the conflict and **reopen the Strait of Hormuz** by Friday. This monumental agreement will kick off a 60-day negotiation period focusing on Iran's nuclear program and regional security.

**💰 Market Mojo & Central Bank Chatter! 📈📉**
Bond markets have been on a wild ride, directly mirroring the Middle East drama. US Treasury yields eased to 4.44% today, their lowest since May 2026, as inflation fears cooled. Earlier in the week, European bond yields fell after the **ECB hiked its deposit rate by 25 basis points to 2.25%** last Thursday (June 11), its first hike since 2023, due to energy-driven inflation. The ECB has revised its inflation forecasts upward and signaled a further September hike. The US dollar softened as peace hopes emerged, with the EUR briefly rising above 1.1550 earlier in the week. This week is packed with central bank action: the **Fed's June 16-17 policy meeting** is concluding today, the **Bank of Japan raised rates to 1%** this week (highest in 30 years), and the Bank of England is expected to hold steady.

Oil prices were the ultimate barometer for Middle East tensions. After soaring on initial conflict escalation, with Brent crude trading above $96 a barrel early in the week, prices plummeted over 5% on Tuesday following the peace deal announcement. The US will allow Iran to immediately begin selling oil and fuel under the deal, with sanctions waivers taking effect swiftly. While OPEC+ had announced a production hike of 188,000 barrels per day from July 2026, its impact is largely overshadowed by the Strait's reopening.

**🌾 Fields of Flux: Agribusiness Update! 🚜**
The **USDA's highly anticipated World Agricultural Supply and Demand Estimates (WASDE) report** on June 11 provided some clarity. The 2026-2027 U.S. corn outlook remained largely unchanged, with the season-average farm price at $4.40 per bushel. Soybean supply, use, and price projections were mostly steady. However, the U.S. wheat outlook projected smaller supplies and lower ending stocks, with **winter wheat production forecast to be the lowest since 1965**. Globally, corn and soybean production estimates for South America were raised. Low water levels on the Mississippi River continue to hike shipping costs, and rising global fertilizer and diesel prices still affect production costs, adding strain to farmers.

Central banks continue to treat price dips as buying opportunities.
